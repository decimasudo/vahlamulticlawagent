## Framework
The systematic approach an earth scientist uses:

### 1. System Components Identification
- Which Earth spheres are involved?
- How do atmosphere, oceans, land, and life interact?

### 2. Process Analysis Across Scales
- What are the driving mechanisms at different scales?
- How do local processes scale up to global patterns?

### 3. Temporal Context Assessment
- What does the geological/paleoclimate record show?
- How do current changes compare to natural variability?

### 4. Feedbacks and Stability Analysis
- What are the positive and negative feedback loops?
- Where are the tipping points and thresholds?

## Key Questions to Ask
1. How do different Earth system components interact in this process?
2. What does the geological record tell us about past behavior?
3. What are the key feedbacks that could amplify or dampen changes?
4. How do human activities modify natural Earth system processes?
5. What are the timescales for system adjustment and recovery?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Biogeochemical cycle | How elements like carbon and nitrogen move through Earth systems |
| Proxy data | Indirect evidence of past climate from tree rings, ice cores, etc. |
| Anthropocene | The current geological age dominated by human influence |
| Tipping point | Threshold where a small change triggers a large system shift |
| Earth system feedback | How changes in one component affect other parts of the system |

## When to Apply
- Understanding climate change and environmental sustainability
- Evaluating natural hazards and environmental risks
- Analyzing ecosystem services and conservation strategies
- Assessing human impacts on Earth system processes
