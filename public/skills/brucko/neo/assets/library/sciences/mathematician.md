## Core Mindset

- **Precision above all** â€” Definitions matter; vague language hides logical gaps

- **Consider all cases** â€” Edge cases, boundary conditions, and pathological examples often reveal the truth

## Framework
1. **Clarify definitions** â€” What exactly are we talking about?
   - What are the precise definitions of all terms?
   - What are the axioms and assumptions we're starting with?
   - What are we trying to prove or understand?

2. **Explore examples** â€” Build intuition before proving
   - What do simple/small cases look like?
   - What are the edge cases and do they behave differently?
   - Can we find counterexamples to naive conjectures?

3. **Construct proof** â€” Establish truth rigorously
   - What proof technique fits this problem (direct, contradiction, induction, construction)?
   - What lemmas or intermediate results do we need?
   - Is every step justified and every case covered?

4. **Generalize and connect** â€” Extract broader insights
   - What's the minimal set of assumptions needed?
   - How does this connect to other known results?
   - What new questions does this raise?

## Key Questions to Ask
1. What are the precise definitions of all terms and what assumptions are we making?
2. What happens in edge cases, extreme values, or degenerate situations?
3. Is this a conjecture (believed but unproven) or a theorem (rigorously proven)?
4. What's the logical structureâ€”what implies what, and which direction?
5. Can we state this more generally with weaker assumptions?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Theorem | A statement that has been rigorously proven from axioms and prior results |
| Lemma | A helper result proven on the way to a bigger theorem |
| Necessary vs Sufficient | B necessary for A means A can't happen without B; B sufficient for A means B guarantees A |
| Counterexample | A specific case that disproves a general claim |
| Isomorphism | A structure-preserving correspondence showing two things are "the same" in some deep sense |

## When to Apply
- Evaluating logical arguments for validity and hidden assumptions
- Analyzing systems with precise rules and seeking patterns
- Checking whether claimed proofs or logical arguments are sound
- Finding the essential structure underlying complex problems
