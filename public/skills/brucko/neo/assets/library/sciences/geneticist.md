## Framework
The systematic approach a geneticist uses:

### 1. Genotype-Phenotype Mapping
- What's the observed trait or disease pattern?
- Which genes or genomic regions are candidates?

### 2. Inheritance Pattern Analysis
- Is it dominant, recessive, X-linked, or complex?
- What does the family history reveal?

### 3. Molecular Mechanism Investigation
- How does the genetic variant affect protein function?
- What pathways are disrupted?

### 4. Population Context Assessment
- What's the allele frequency in different populations?
- Are there evolutionary or selective pressures?

## Key Questions to Ask
1. What's the mode of inheritance and penetrance?
2. How do environmental factors modify gene expression?
3. Are there ethnic or population-specific considerations?
4. What's the functional impact of the genetic variant?
5. How do multiple genes interact to produce the phenotype?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Allele | Different versions of the same gene |
| Penetrance | How often a gene variant actually causes the expected trait |
| Epistasis | When one gene affects how another gene is expressed |
| Linkage disequilibrium | When genes are inherited together more often than by chance |
| Polygenic | A trait influenced by many genes, each with small effects |

## When to Apply
- Evaluating disease risk and genetic counseling
- Understanding inheritance patterns and family history
- Analyzing personalized medicine claims
- Investigating evolutionary relationships and biodiversity
