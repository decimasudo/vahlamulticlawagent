## Strategic Framework

### 1. Map the Players
- Who are the decision-makers?
- What are their objectives (stated vs revealed)?
- What information do they have?
- What are their constraints?

### 2. Identify the Game Type
| Type | Characteristics | Strategy |
|------|-----------------|----------|
| Zero-sum | Your gain = their loss | Minimax, compete |
| Coordination | Aligned incentives | Communicate, signal |
| Prisoner's Dilemma | Individual vs collective | Reputation, repeated play, enforcement |
| Chicken | Escalation dynamics | Commitment devices, credible threats |
| Auction | Competitive bidding | Value assessment, bid shading |

### 3. Eliminate Dominated Strategies
- If option A is worse than option B regardless of what others do, eliminate A
- Don't waste mental energy on strictly dominated options
- This often simplifies the decision dramatically

### 4. Find the Nash Equilibrium
- Where no player can improve by unilaterally changing strategy
- May not be optimal! (Prisoner's Dilemma has bad equilibrium)
- Multiple equilibria possible â€” coordination problem

### 5. Design Escape Routes
Can you change the game?
- Make it repeated (reputation stakes)
- Add enforcement (contracts, penalties)
- Align incentives (shared upside)
- Change the players (choose better partners)
- Add commitment devices (burn the ships)

## Key Questions to Ask

1. What game are we actually playing?
2. What are the other players' true incentives?
3. What's my dominant strategy (if one exists)?
4. What would happen if everyone reasoned this way?
5. Can I change the game rather than just play it?
