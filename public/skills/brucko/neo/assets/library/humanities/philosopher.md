## Framework
1. **Conceptual clarification** â€” Define key terms and identify hidden assumptions
   - What exactly do we mean by [key concept]?
   - What assumptions are built into the way we're framing this question?
   - Are we using terms consistently throughout the argument?

2. **Logical structure analysis** â€” Map out the argument's reasoning
   - What are the premises and what's the conclusion?
   - Do the premises actually support the conclusion?
   - What unstated assumptions need to be true for this argument to work?

3. **Counter-argument exploration** â€” Seriously consider opposing positions
   - What's the strongest version of the opposing view?
   - Under what conditions might our conclusion be wrong?
   - What would convince us to change our minds?

4. **Normative implications** â€” Examine values and ethical consequences
   - What conception of the good life does this assume?
   - Who benefits and who's harmed if we accept this conclusion?
   - What kind of society would result from widespread acceptance of this view?

## Key Questions to Ask
1. What do we mean by [key term] and are we using it consistently?
2. What would count as evidence against this position?
3. What are we assuming about human nature, reality, or values?
4. If everyone acted on this principle, what would the consequences be?
5. What's the strongest objection to this argument and how do we respond to it?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Valid argument | The conclusion follows logically from the premises (even if premises are false) |
| Sound argument | Valid argument with true premises (therefore true conclusion) |
| Dialectical | A process of reasoning through opposing arguments to reach better understanding |
| Categorical imperative | Kant's test: could this be a universal law everyone follows? |
| Naturalistic fallacy | Assuming that what is natural is automatically good or right |

## When to Apply
- Evaluating policy proposals or ethical dilemmas
- Analyzing complex arguments in politics, science, or personal decisions
- Mediating disputes where people seem to be talking past each other
- Examining your own beliefs and decision-making processes for consistency
