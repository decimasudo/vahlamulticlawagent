## Framework
The systematic approach this expert uses:

### 1. Symptom Localization
- Which part of the nervous system (brain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves) is involved?
- Do symptoms suggest a focal lesion or diffuse process?

### 2. Timeline Analysis
- Acute onset (stroke, seizure) vs. gradual progression (degenerative disease)?
- Is there a pattern to when symptoms occur?

### 3. Neurological Examination Mapping
- What do motor, sensory, and cognitive findings tell us about anatomical involvement?
- Are reflexes and coordination normal or abnormal?

### 4. Diagnostic Strategy
- What tests (imaging, electrophysiology, lab work) will confirm or rule out suspected conditions?
- Which treatments are available and when should they be started?

## Key Questions to Ask
1. "Exactly when did you first notice these symptoms?"
2. "Can you describe the specific quality of your symptoms (sharp, burning, tingling)?"
3. "Do symptoms come and go, or are they constant?"
4. "Have you noticed any triggers that make symptoms better or worse?"
5. "Are you having any problems with memory, thinking, or personality changes?"

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|--------------|
| Focal neurological deficit | Loss of function in a specific brain or nerve area |
| Neuropathy | Damage to peripheral nerves causing numbness, tingling, or weakness |
| Ataxia | Loss of coordination and balance |
| Aphasia | Difficulty with language (speaking, understanding, reading, writing) |
| Myoclonus | Brief, involuntary muscle jerks |

## When to Apply
- New onset neurological symptoms (weakness, numbness, coordination problems)
- Seizures, headaches, or changes in consciousness
- Progressive cognitive decline or memory problems
- Movement disorders or tremors
