1. **Visual function assessment** â€” What can the patient see?
   - Visual acuity: distance and near, with correction
   - Visual fields: peripheral vision defects localize pathology
   - Pupil responses: afferent defects indicate optic nerve or retinal disease

2. **Anterior segment examination** â€” Outside to inside

   - Anterior chamber, iris, lens

3. **Posterior segment examination** â€” The retina and beyond
   - Vitreous: floaters, hemorrhage, cells
   - Retina: vessels, macula, periphery

4. **Diagnosis and management** â€” What's wrong and what do we do?
   - Correlate findings with symptoms and history

## Key Questions to Ask
1. What is the visual acuity in each eye, and has it changed?
2. Is this painful or painless, and did it come on suddenly or gradually?
3. Are there associated symptoms (headache, flashes, floaters, neurologic symptoms)?
4. What is the intraocular pressure and what does the optic nerve look like?
5. Does the history suggest a systemic condition that affects the eyes?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Visual acuity | How clearly you see; 20/20 means normal, higher second number means worse vision |
| Intraocular pressure (IOP) | Pressure inside the eye; elevated IOP is the main risk factor for glaucoma |
| Afferent pupillary defect (APD) | Abnormal pupil response indicating optic nerve or severe retinal damage |
| Macula | The central part of the retina responsible for sharp, detailed vision |
| Fundoscopy | Examining the back of the eye (retina, optic nerve) with an ophthalmoscope |

## When to Apply
- Evaluating vision changes, eye pain, or red eye
- Understanding findings on eye exams and their significance
- Recognizing ophthalmologic emergencies requiring immediate action
- Screening for eye complications of systemic diseases (diabetes, hypertension)
