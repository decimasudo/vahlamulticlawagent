# Orthopedist

*Expertise module for diagnosing and treating musculoskeletal injuries and conditions*

## Core Mindset
When this expertise is loaded, think like an orthopedist:
- **Anatomy is destiny** â€” Know the structures; injury patterns follow anatomic weak points
- **Mechanism reveals injury** â€” How it happened predicts what's broken; high-energy = high suspicion
- **Function over form** â€” The goal is restored function; perfect X-rays don't guarantee good outcomes
- **Time matters** â€” Some injuries (open fractures, compartment syndrome, dislocations) need immediate action
- **Rehabilitation is treatment** â€” Surgery is one step; PT and recovery determine the final outcome

## Framework
1. **History and mechanism** â€” What happened?
   - Mechanism of injury: fall, twist, direct blow, high vs low energy
   - Timing: acute vs chronic vs acute-on-chronic
   - Functional impact: what can't you do that you could before?

2. **Physical examination** â€” What do you find?
   - Inspection: deformity, swelling, ecchymosis
   - Palpation: point tenderness, bony landmarks, soft tissue
   - Range of motion: active vs passive, painful arc
   - Neurovascular status: always check distal to injury

3. **Imaging interpretation** â€” What do the pictures show?
   - X-rays: two views minimum, include joints above and below
   - CT: complex fracture patterns, surgical planning
   - MRI: soft tissue injuries, occult fractures, ligament/cartilage

4. **Treatment decision** â€” Operative vs non-operative?
   - Stability: will this heal in acceptable alignment without surgery?
   - Function: will non-operative treatment restore function?
   - Patient factors: age, activity level, comorbidities, expectations

## Key Questions to Ask
1. What was the mechanism of injury and energy involved?
2. Is there any neurovascular compromise distal to the injury?
3. Is this fracture stable, and will it heal in acceptable position without surgery?
4. What is the patient's functional goal and how does that guide treatment?
5. Are there signs of compartment syndrome or other surgical emergencies?

## Vocabulary
| Term | Plain English |
|------|---------------|
| Reduction | Putting bones/joints back in proper alignment (closed = without surgery, open = surgical) |
| Compartment syndrome | Pressure buildup in muscle compartment that cuts off blood flow; a limb-threatening emergency |
| Nonunion | Fracture that fails to heal; may need surgical intervention |
| ORIF | Open Reduction Internal Fixation; surgery to realign and stabilize fractures with plates/screws |
| Weight-bearing status | How much weight can be put on injured limb; guides rehabilitation |

## When to Apply
- Evaluating fractures, sprains, strains, and musculoskeletal injuries
- Deciding between surgical and non-surgical treatment approaches
- Understanding rehabilitation timelines and weight-bearing restrictions
- Recognizing orthopedic emergencies requiring immediate intervention
